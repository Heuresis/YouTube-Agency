11 service-business founders. Same problem: trapped in delivery. 10 tried to get out. 1 actually did.

[CUT to on-camera, direct.]

[CUT. SUPER: "what was different?"]

## Contrast Setup (0:20 - 1:00)

[VISUAL: split-screen continues. SUPER labels each side with anonymized initials.]

11 operators. All running service businesses between $200K and $500K revenue. All personally producing 70-80% of delivery work. All hitting the same ceiling — they couldn't grow without burning out.

[CUT.]

I followed them over 18 months. Same starting position. Same pain. Different outcomes.

The 10 ran a familiar pattern: hire, struggle, revert. Hire, struggle, revert. Hire someone better. Still struggle. Still revert.

The 1 ran a different sequence entirely.

## Beats — Developing Contrast (1:00 - 4:30)

[Beat 1 — what the 10 did]

[VISUAL: 10-side highlighted. B-ROLL: stock footage of busy office.]

The 10 started with hiring. They identified the work that was draining them. They found someone capable. They hired. They onboarded. The hire did the work for 60-90 days. Then the hire quit, or the operator stepped back in to "fix" something, or the work quality dropped enough that the operator pulled it back into their own hands.

[CUT.]

What looked like a hiring problem was actually upstream of the hire. By the time the hire walked in, there was no role for them to walk into. The work the operator was doing wasn't designed to be done by someone else. So the hire couldn't actually own it.

[Beat 2 — what the 1 did]

[VISUAL: 1-side highlighted. B-ROLL: hand drawing on whiteboard.]

The 1 didn't hire first. She designed the role first. She wrote down — before any hire was on the radar — what the role would own, what decisions it would make, what it would escalate, what its outputs would look like.

[CUT.]

Once the role existed on paper as a thing-to-own, not a list-of-tasks-to-do, she hired. The person who walked in had a job that already existed. They knew what they owned. They knew what they decided. They knew when to escalate. There was no founder-attached confusion because the role wasn't tied to the founder's preferences moment-to-moment.

[Beat 3 — the structural difference]

[VISUAL: side-by-side comparison. SUPER: "task vs role"]

The 10 delegated tasks. The 1 delegated a role. Tasks need a hand. Roles need an owner. The 10 hired hands. The 1 hired an owner.

[CUT.]

The fix was sequence: design first, hire second. Not "hire smart" — "design smart, then hire."

[Beat 4 — the SOP layer]

[VISUAL: 10-side / 1-side split. SUPER: "SOPs vs ownership"]

The 10 also tried to fix delegation by writing SOPs. The SOPs sat unused. Why? Because nobody owned the SOP. The operator wrote it. The hire was supposed to follow it. Nobody updated it. Nobody decided when to deviate.

The 1 made the SOP the role's responsibility. The role-owner updated it. The role-owner decided when to deviate. The SOP became a living system instead of a frozen document.

## Twist / Reveal (4:30 - 5:00)

[VISUAL: dramatic CUT. SUPER: "the surprise"]

Here's what's surprising about the 1 vs the 10: the 1 wasn't a better operator. She didn't have more experience. She wasn't smarter or harder-working.

[CUT.]

The thing that separated her was that she stopped trying to fix the symptom (the hire) and looked at the upstream system (the role). The 10 kept fixing the hire. She fixed the structure.

It's not a personality difference. It's a sequence difference.

## Close (5:00 - 6:00)

The pattern across the 10 was: hire → fail → conclude they need a better hire → repeat. The pattern from the 1 was: design → hire → review → adjust.

[CUT.]

The takeaway is implicit. If you're cycling through hires, the problem is probably upstream of who you hire.
