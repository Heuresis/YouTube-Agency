# YouTube Community

> The Community tab. The fourth pillar of the channel's surface area, alongside long-form, Shorts, and Live. This document covers post-type strategy, cadence ranges, cross-platform community (channel + email + Discord/Skool/Circle), and the comment-tab cadence.

## What the Community tab is in 2026

The Community tab is the channel's text-and-image post surface. Available to channels above 500 subscribers (the threshold has shifted over the years). Posts appear in the Community tab on the channel page, and selectively in subscribers' home feeds. Post types:

- **Text post** — up to ~1,500 characters, plain or with minor formatting
- **Image post** — single image with optional caption
- **Multi-image post** — carousel-style, multiple images with caption
- **Poll** — multiple-choice poll with text or image options
- **Quiz** — single-correct-answer with explanation
- **Video clip** — short clip embedded as a post (less commonly used)
- **GIF post** — animated image (rarely used outside lifestyle / entertainment)

Each type has different mechanics and different audience response patterns.

## Why Community matters

Three structural reasons:

### 1. Pillar of cross-platform attention

The Community tab is the channel's text-and-image surface. It complements the video formats. Without it, the channel is video-only — the operator cannot reach subscribers between uploads, cannot build conversation, cannot test ideas, cannot run lightweight reach-mode content.

### 2. Audience-research instrument

Polls and questions surface the audience's voice. "Which topic do you want next?" "Which framework helped you most?" "What's your current bottleneck?" The responses feed `/idea-farm`, `/audience-researcher`'s work, and `/research-brief`. Without Community, audience research is heavily dependent on comments and DMs (which are less structured).

### 3. Algorithm signal

Active Community engagement signals the algorithm that the channel has an engaged audience, which marginally lifts the surfacing of new uploads. Not the dominant signal — but cumulatively meaningful.

## Post-type strategy

### Polls

The highest-engagement post type for most channels. Used for:

- **Audience research** — "Which topic do you want next?"
- **Belief surfacing** — "Which framework do you use?" (reveals current beliefs)
- **Engagement primer** — easy-tap engagement before a major upload

Cadence: 1–2 polls per week typical for active channels. More than 2/week dilutes engagement per poll.

### Image posts

Used for:

- **Behind-the-scenes content** — production stills, location shots
- **Quote / framework graphics** — pull-quotes from videos, framework diagrams
- **Soft case-study posts** — anonymized client wins (without violating privacy)
- **Channel-update visuals** — banners for upcoming videos, course launches, events

Cadence: 1–3 image posts per week typical. More than 3/week dilutes attention.

### Text posts

Used for:

- **Long-form thought / essay-shorter** — a reflection that could be its own video at smaller scale
- **Channel updates** — what's coming, what shifted, what the operator is thinking
- **Question-to-audience** — "What's your take on X?" (open-ended community engagement)
- **Cross-promotion** — pointing to a new long-form, a podcast appearance, a guest interview

Cadence: 2–4 text posts per week for active channels.

### Multi-image / carousel posts

Used for:

- **Step-by-step guides** — the same content as a long-form video, compressed into 5–7 image cards
- **Before/after sequences** — visual case studies
- **Framework explainer** — each image walks through one part of the framework

Cadence: 1–2 multi-image posts per week, when the topic warrants the format.

### Video clip posts

Less common in 2026 — Shorts has largely displaced this use case. Video clips on Community are typically used for:

- **Behind-the-scenes** — short clips of production, team, lifestyle
- **Members-only teasers** — clips visible to members for cohort engagement

### Quiz posts

Used selectively for educational engagement. The audience taps an answer, sees the explanation. Works well in education niches; less in entertainment.

## Cadence ranges

For a channel with a long-form anchor:

- **3–6 Community posts per week** is the typical sustainable range
- **Daily posts** is achievable but operationally heavy; the per-post engagement typically declines if the audience can't keep up
- **Weekly posts** is below the leverage point; the algorithm de-prioritizes Community surface for low-cadence channels

A reasonable mix for a 4-posts-per-week cadence:

- Monday: poll (audience research / engagement primer for the week's upload)
- Wednesday: image post (framework graphic / behind-the-scenes / case study tease)
- Friday: text post (reflection / industry thought / cross-promotion)
- Sunday: image post (week-recap / community-shoutout / next-week tease)

The cadence is calibrated to the channel's content cycle, not a rigid schedule.

## Cross-platform community

The Community tab is one of several owned-audience surfaces. The full architecture:

### Channel surfaces

- **Long-form library** — the deep-attention asset
- **Shorts feed** — the broad-reach asset
- **Live streams** — the real-time asset
- **Community tab** — the asynchronous text/image asset

### Owned-audience surfaces

- **Email list** — the most-owned audience surface; sender controls delivery; no platform algorithm
- **Discord / Skool / Circle / private community** — high-engagement, high-friction audience; small but deep
- **SMS list** — emerging in 2026; very high open rate, very high friction

### Cross-platform routing

The audience moves across these surfaces over time. A new viewer enters via long-form or Shorts → subscribes to the channel → engages with Community posts → joins the email list → joins the private community → buys the back-end offer. This is the audience-building loop (compartment 9).

The Community tab's role in the loop: it bridges from the channel surface (passive viewer) to the email list (active subscriber). Polls and questions on Community generate signals that justify the email-list invitation. Channel-update text posts that announce new lead magnets push the bridge.

## Comment-tab cadence

The comments-tab below each video is the channel's third community surface (alongside the dedicated Community tab and the chat in live streams).

Comment-engagement strategies:

### First-hour-only

The operator engages with comments only in the first hour after publish. This concentrates engagement when the algorithm is making early-surfacing decisions. After the first hour, comments are managed by team or unattended. The first-hour-only strategy is the highest-leverage approach for solo operators.

### Pinned-replies

The operator pins their own reply or a high-signal comment to the top of the comment section. The pinned comment becomes a primary call-to-action surface (lead magnet, pinned VSL link, application form). The pinned comment's CTR is typically 3–8x higher than description links.

### Full engagement

The operator (or team) engages with most or all comments. Highest engagement signal but operationally heavy. Sustainable for small channels (<50K subs); impractical at scale unless team-driven.

### Minimal

The operator rarely engages with comments. Lower engagement signal but lower operations cost. Acceptable for some authority channels where the operator's parasocial trust is established and comment-engagement isn't the trust mechanic.

The comment-tab cadence is set in `company.yaml.audience_building_loop.community_strategy.comment_reply_strategy`.

## Comment capture protocol

High-signal comments — those that contain audience pain language, voice-of-viewer phrases, contrarian feedback, case-study leads, content-idea suggestions — should be captured for downstream use:

- **Voice-of-viewer phrases** → compartment 2 (audience intelligence) → script-writer reads
- **Content ideas** → `/idea-farm` backlog
- **Pain language patterns** → script-writer's hook bank
- **Case-study leads** → outreach for interview / case study
- **Contrarian feedback** → considered for inclusion or rebuttal in a future video

The community-manager role owns comment capture. Volume is high; quality is variable. The capture protocol filters for signal.

## Engagement-bait ban

INV-17 applies on the Community tab. Banned post patterns:

- "Comment YES if you…"
- "Tap subscribe to win…"
- "Type X in the comments…"
- "This will BLOW your mind…"
- Manufactured outrage, fake-stakes, fake personal-event-just-happened framings

The Community tab is high-velocity; engagement-bait is tempting because it spikes engagement metrics. INV-17 forbids it. The trust contract erodes faster than the engagement spike compounds.

## Genuine engagement prompts

Acceptable patterns (genuine vs. manufactured):

- "What's your take on X?" — genuine, open-ended
- "Which of these worked for you?" (with multiple-choice options) — poll-based audience research
- "Drop your best [topic-tip] in the replies" — collaborative, value-rich
- "Tell me which framework you want next" — content direction request

Genuine engagement is on-brand. Manufactured engagement breaks INV-17.

## Community-tab as a pillar

For channels with developed audience infrastructure, the Community tab is treated as a primary pillar — not an afterthought. Cadence is planned weekly. Post types are mixed strategically. Engagement is monitored. The Community tab feeds the email list, which feeds the back-end offer.

For channels without developed audience infrastructure, the Community tab is an underused surface. The OS resists letting it stay underused — `/community-cadence` skill builds the cadence into the channel's operations.

## Cross-platform community (Discord / Skool / Circle)

For channels with a private community alongside the YouTube Community tab, the architecture is layered:

- **YouTube Community tab** — public, low-friction, broad audience
- **Email list** — opt-in, owned, broader than the private community
- **Private community (Discord / Skool / Circle)** — invite or paid, high-engagement, narrow audience

Each surface's content differs:

- **YouTube Community**: lightweight engagement, polls, snapshots, audience research
- **Email**: deeper teaching, longer-form thought, calls-to-action
- **Private community**: peer engagement, high-trust conversation, content the operator can't or won't make public

Routing between them: the YouTube Community tab points to the email list; the email list points to the private community; the private community points back to the long-form library. The loop closes.
