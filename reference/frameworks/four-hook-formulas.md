# The Four Hook Formulas

## Origin archetype
Educational-creator script architect synthesizing million-view openers across multiple high-ticket niches.

## What it solves
Generic hooks (e.g., "today I'm going to talk about...") fail because they don't pass the 3-second survival-brain check. Each of these four formulas is a structurally complete answer to "what does the first sentence have to do."

## Structure

### Formula A — PAST Method (Problem / Agitate / Solution-tease / Tease-delivery)

**Structure:**
1. Problem callout — name the specific painful challenge the viewer is facing.
2. Agitate — amplify the cost and frustration of not solving it.
3. Solution tease — promise a unique method or framework.
4. Tease delivery — create anticipation and explicitly delay immediate gratification.

**Generic structural example:** "If you're struggling to [achieve specific outcome] and feeling like maybe [self-defeating belief], I have news. I discovered [specific method] that [specific result tied to numbers]. In the next [X] minutes, I'll break it down step-by-step. But first, let me show you why [common conventional advice] is actually [hidden cost]..."

### Formula B — AID Method (Attention / Interest / Desire / Delay)

**Structure:**
1. Attention — open with a curiosity-inducing unanswered question.
2. Interest — dismiss a common belief, introduce a new perspective.
3. Desire — promise a specific valuable outcome.
4. Delay — use "but first..." to postpone the satisfaction.

**Generic structural example:** "Have you ever wondered why some [things in your space] [achieve impossible outcome] while [perfectly executed alternatives] [fail]? You've probably heard it's [common explanation]. But what if it has nothing to do with [common explanation] and everything to do with [contrarian truth]? Once you understand this, [transformation will follow]. But before I reveal it, I need to explain why [setup belief]..."

### Formula C — Contrast Hook

**Structure:** Unexpected past action → surprising present result. Place the contrast in the same sentence; let the audience's brain fill the gap.

**Generic structural example:** "In [year], I [did the unexpected thing]. This year, that same [unexpected thing] is [impressive present result]..."

### Formula D — Shocking Statistic Hook

**Structure:** Relatable smaller numbers → surprising aggregated total → stakes for the viewer's situation.

**Generic structural example:** "If you spend [$X on common item], [$Y on another common item], and [$Z on a third common item] every month, that's [shocking annual total]. This is the reality for [target avatar] — money / time / energy just disappearing..."

## When to use
- **PAST:** when the audience is solution-aware and the pain is well-known. Best for established niches with clear ICP language.
- **AID:** when the audience is problem-aware but solution-aware audiences will accept misleading "everything you know is wrong" framing. Use sparingly.
- **Contrast:** when the operator has a personal story with a high-status credential they can downgrade. Strongest authority hook in the family.
- **Statistic:** when the niche is populated by people who underestimate a hidden cost (subscriptions, churn, time-leak). Lowest emotion-load, highest analytical-load.

## Why each works
- PAST exploits the brain's hardwired bias toward unsolved problems (a survival mechanism).
- AID exploits the dismissal-then-reframe pattern (the brain rewards information that feels new or contrarian).
- Contrast exploits the prediction-error response — the brain works hardest to fill in surprising gaps.
- Statistic exploits the aggregation surprise — small numbers compose into a number the conscious mind never tracks.

## Variants observed
Some operators add a 5th "Contrarian" hook (see Contrarian Angle Formula) and a 6th "Curiosity Stack" hook (multi-element). These are retained as separate frameworks downstream because their structural logic is materially different from PAST/AID/Contrast/Statistic.
