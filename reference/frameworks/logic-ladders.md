# Logic Ladders (A→B→C→D→Z Reasoning Chains)

## Origin archetype
Direct-response-tradition script architect porting copywriting's "reasons-why" technique into long-form video.

## What it solves
"If you post on YouTube, you can make $100,000 per month" — viewers don't believe it because the logic ladder is missing. They cannot fill in the steps from "post a video" to "$100k/month" themselves, so they dismiss the claim as fantasy.

## Structure — explicit step-by-step chain

When you post YouTube videos with proper frameworks
↓
You generate traffic and views
↓
That traffic watches your content
↓
They click the link in description
↓
They watch your landing page
↓
They book a call or buy
↓
You close the sale
↓
Money in pocket
↓
Therefore: YouTube video → Money.

## Application rule
Use logic ladders to explain any claim that might seem unbelievable. Connect every dot. The audience cannot dismiss a claim if they have to dismiss every link in the chain — and chains of 5+ links are nearly impossible to dismiss because each link is individually plausible.

## Two operational sub-types

**Trust ladder** (why should they trust you):
"Why should you trust me? ↓ I [credential 1] for [N years] ↓ Generated [specific outcome] ↓ My [team / clients / students] [drove specific impressive metric] ↓ We've [aggregated outcome over time] ↓ Therefore: I know what I'm talking about."

**Urgency ladder** (why should they act now):
"Why should you start today? ↓ Everyone is doing [X] now ↓ More people = more competition ↓ Competition will only increase ↓ Time in the game cannot be replaced ↓ Therefore: start today or fall behind."

## When to use
Every claim the viewer's first reaction would be "that's not how it works" or "I don't see how that connects." Belief installation depends on the audience never being able to skip a step in the chain.

## Why it works
The brain's default move when faced with an implausible claim is to *dismiss the conclusion*. The brain's much harder move is to *dismiss every individual step*. Logic ladders force the brain into the harder mode, where dismissal requires real effort, which the brain doesn't expend on a casual scroll-past video.

## Variants observed
Some operators teach 3-step ladders; others teach 10-step ladders. Canonical form retained: 5–8 steps, because shorter ladders fail to neutralize the dismiss-the-conclusion default and longer ladders cross into "this is suspicious / why is the operator over-justifying" territory.
