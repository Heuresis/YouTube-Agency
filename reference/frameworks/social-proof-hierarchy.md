# Social Proof Hierarchy

## Origin archetype
Long-form-VSL specialist running 8-figure-tested social-proof rotations.

## What it solves
Not all social proof is equal. Generic testimonials are nearly useless; detailed case studies are nearly always overweighted. The hierarchy tells you which proof to use and in which density.

## Structure — power ranking from strongest to weakest

| Rank | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (BEST) | Detailed case study with before/after journey | Highest trust signal; one of these beats 100 generic testimonials |
| 2 | Specific result testimonial with numbers | Quantified outcome required |
| 3 | Skeptical-convert testimonial | "I almost didn't buy..." pattern; pre-handles the viewer's own skepticism |
| 4 | Multiple customer results aggregated | Density-driven; "147 students hit [result]" |
| 5 | Media mentions and press | Third-party authority |
| 6 | Expert endorsements | External credibility |
| 7 (WEAKEST) | Generic positive testimonials | Effectively dismissed by audience |

## Operating rule
"One detailed case study beats 100 generic testimonials. Specificity and measurable outcomes over volume."

## Reconciling the apparent contradiction with the "social proof spam" instruction in the Six Blockage Diagnostic
Spam *high-grade* proof (rank 1–2). Don't spam low-grade proof (rank 7). Volume of high-grade proof is multiplicative; volume of low-grade proof is additive at best, distracting at worst. The operator who shows 10 detailed case studies wins; the operator who shows 100 generic testimonials loses against the operator showing zero.

## When to use
Every belief-installation moment in the script (Eight Required Beliefs, Belief 3 and Belief 8). Also: every testimonial section in the 15-step VSL (Step 8).

## Why it works
The viewer's social-proof receptor is calibrated to specificity, not volume. Generic praise reads as either fake or as "the speaker's friends being polite"; specific, quantified, before-and-after journeys read as actual evidence the speaker's method produces results in the world.

## Variants observed
Some operators use a 5-tier hierarchy (compressing types 4–6); others use a 9-tier extended version (adding "skeptic-to-evangelist" and "competitor-converted" tiers). The 7-tier form retained here is the minimum-set that captures the operationally-important distinctions.
