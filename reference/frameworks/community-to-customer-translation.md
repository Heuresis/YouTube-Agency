# Community-to-Customer Translation

## Origin archetype
Community-design practice fused with direct-response funnel architecture: each community signal mapped to a specific conversion artifact by funnel builders.

## What it solves
Community gravity is the source; back-end revenue is the downstream. Most creators build one or the other and lose the compounding: a channel with real community markers but a generic funnel leaves the gravity unconverted; a polished funnel with no community underneath converts cold and refunds warm. The translation is mechanical — each of the four community markers (`four-community-markers`) has a specific funnel artifact that captures it. This file is the conversion map.

## Structure — the translation map

| Community marker | Funnel mechanic | Artifacts |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Sacred vocabulary | The phrases become load-bearing copy | Titles, hooks, lead-magnet name, email subjects, VSL section heads, application-page headers, program module names |
| 2. Binary in-vs-out | Positioning + the disqualifier list | "Who this is for / not for" block; named-category contrast section |
| 3. Shared ritual | Content cadence + cohort cadence | Upload rhythm, community-tab rhythm, named cohort-open windows, post-purchase rhythm |
| 4. Mission over transaction | Brand voice + the creator manifesto | About page, VSL mission block, inclusion-stack narrative, mission-framed guarantee |

## Translation 1 — Sacred vocabulary → copy phrases
The coined phrases are not decorative; they are the funnel's connective tissue. A viewer who learned "the [coined term] problem" from free videos and then meets the same phrase as the VSL's first section header experiences the funnel as *one continuous conversation* — which is what makes the ask feel earned rather than switched-on.

**Where the phrases land:**

| Surface | Role |
|---|---|
| Video titles + hooks | Recognition at the feed layer; the phrase IS the promise |
| Lead-magnet name | The free artifact is named in the vocabulary ("The [Coined Term] Audit") |
| Email subject lines | Re-invokes the vocabulary at the inbox sort moment |
| VSL section headers | The mechanism carries its coined name throughout |
| Application page + booking confirmation | Vocabulary at peak commitment moments |
| Program module names | The vocabulary becomes the deliverable's structure |

**Rule of 5:** pick the five phrases that must appear everywhere; they are non-negotiable per asset. **Audit test:** pull the last 30 days of uploads + the VSL + the application page; each phrase should appear multiple times per surface. **Anti-pattern:** writing fresh copy per asset — fresh copy resets the compounding clock; write the phrases once, reuse, refine.

## Translation 2 — Binary in-vs-out → positioning + disqualifier list
The in/out dynamic becomes two copy structures:

**The "for / not-for" block** — placed at the VSL close, the application page, and the channel about page:

```
This program is for:
- [Niche avatar] at [stage] with [constraint]
- Creators who already [minimum behavior — e.g., publish weekly]
- People who want [the outcome the mechanism actually delivers]

This program is NOT for:
- [Earlier-stage case] (the free playlist covers this — start there)
- [Adjacent avatar the mechanism doesn't fit] (a different approach wins for you)
- Anyone who needs [thing the program doesn't provide]
```

The NOT-for list is non-negotiable. It is the binary made copy, it pre-filters refund-prone buyers, and it lowers perceived pressure — which *raises* close rates among the fits.

**The named-category contrast section** — the three-beat structure, run in the VSL and on the application page:
1. **Old category:** what the dominant approach does, named specifically as an approach.
2. **Why it fails structurally:** the mismatch with this viewer's situation — mechanism, not morality.
3. **New approach:** the creator's named system and why its structure fits.

**Ethical constraint:** the old category is a *category of approach*, never a named person or channel (`parasocial-ethical-guardrails`, Red Flag 3).

## Translation 3 — Shared ritual → cadence architecture
Ritual at the community level becomes three designed cadences at the funnel level:

1. **Pre-offer cadence (the warm-up).** The new viewer's first 14 days: the weekly upload rhythm, 2–3 community posts, the lead magnet's welcome sequence. Target: by day 14 the viewer has met the five sacred phrases several times each and knows the channel's rhythm. This is `lead-magnet-bridge` territory.
2. **Launch / cohort cadence (the ritualized open).** Enrollment opens on a nameable rhythm — fixed windows, fixed caps with the structural reason stated, no quiet extensions (an extended "deadline" teaches the audience that deadlines are theater, and burns INV-16 trust). Pre-open content references the window; a post-cohort debrief shows aggregate results (real ones — INV-6).
3. **Post-purchase cadence (the retention rhythm).** Onboarding sequence, fixed check-in rhythm, visible milestone moments, renewal/expansion conversation on a known schedule. This cadence is what turns a one-time buyer into a renewal — the buyer inherits the habit the channel already taught them.

**The sacred-cadence move:** name one recurring moment per surface and defend it for a year. When audience members self-schedule around it, ritual is operational.

## Translation 4 — Mission → manifesto + mission-framed assets
The mission lives in three funnel places:

1. **The creator manifesto** — not a bio. One page: what the creator saw in the niche that was wrong → why it matters beyond the sale → what they're building instead → who it's for and not for. Linked from the channel about page, the VSL, and the application confirmation. It converts skeptical, sophisticated viewers (Stage 4–5 niches per `market-sophistication-five-stages`) at a visibly higher rate than credentials do.
2. **The VSL mission block** — placed after the mechanism walkthrough, before the CTA: "here's what this is actually about," one story of a person the mission served (real, permissioned), the stakes for the niche, then the ask in mission-charged language. Low-sophistication audiences don't need it; high-sophistication audiences notice its absence.
3. **The inclusion-stack narrative** — the program's components framed as mission-delivery, not bonuses: "because the mission is [X], the program also includes [Y]." Each inclusion answers a *because*. A guarantee phrased in mission language ("the point is your system working — if [measurable checkpoint] isn't met by [date], [remedy]") outperforms the same terms phrased as financial fine print.

## The translation audit
Run quarterly across the live funnel:
1. **Vocabulary density:** the five phrases counted across last-30-days uploads, the VSL, and the application page.
2. **Binary blocks:** for/not-for present on VSL + application page? Named-category contrast present?
3. **Cadence:** can warm audience members state the upload rhythm, the cohort rhythm, and (for buyers) the post-purchase rhythm?
4. **Mission:** manifesto live and linked? Mission block in the VSL? Inclusions framed as mission-delivery?

4/4 = the funnel is converting the community's gravity. 0–1 = the markers are real but the funnel is generic — the channel is earning trust it never converts, and the gap shows up as "great audience, weak launches."

## Directional impact
Each translation, applied honestly, tends to raise close rate and lower refund/regret rate — and they compound multiplicatively, because each one removes a different discontinuity between the free experience and the paid ask. The mechanism is continuity: every translated surface tells the buyer "this is the same person, the same standard, the same conversation you've been in for months." (Measure the channel's own deltas; do not import claimed percentages — INV-6.)

## When to use
Funnel construction and revision (`lead-magnet-bridge`, `write-pinned-vsl`, `build-application-funnel`), offer packaging (`design-offer` — the inclusion-stack narrative), community-cadence design, and any launch post-mortem where the audience engaged but didn't convert.

## Why it works
A funnel is a trust hand-off, and trust degrades at discontinuities — new vocabulary, new tone, new rhythm, new persona at the exact moment money enters. The four translations remove the discontinuities one by one, so the purchase reads as the *next step in an existing relationship* rather than the start of a sales process. Continuity is also why translated funnels refund less: the buyer got exactly the relationship the channel promised.

## Anti-patterns
- **Funnel-voice switch:** free content in the creator's voice, sales pages in borrowed direct-response hype voice. The discontinuity costs more than the "proven" copy gains (and usually trips INV-7 banned vocabulary).
- **Vocabulary on the funnel only:** coining phrases for the VSL that never appear in content — the password no one was taught.
- **Cohort theater:** fake caps and rolling "last chance" windows — detected once, every future launch underperforms (INV-16).
- **Manifesto inflation:** a mission page that restates benefits. If the mission doesn't visibly constrain a real decision (a sponsor declined, an off-fit buyer turned away), it isn't a mission yet.

## Variants observed
Some operators run the translations only at launch windows. Canonical form retained: standing translation — the funnel carries the community's vocabulary, binary, rhythm, and mission year-round, because trust continuity is a property of the whole system, not of launch copy.
