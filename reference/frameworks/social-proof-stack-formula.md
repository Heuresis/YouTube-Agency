# The Social Proof Stack Formula

## Origin archetype
Title-craft specialist focused on coaching and education niches where social proof at scale is the differentiator.

## What it solves
A single testimonial in a title is weaker than aggregated social proof. The stack formula leverages density.

## Structure — the formula

"[Number] Students Proof This [Strategy] Works for [Audience]"

## Worked structural examples (anonymized)
- "147 Students Proof This Sales Method Works for Introverts"
- "Why 500+ Creators Use This Thumbnail Strategy"
- "1,200 Students Proof This Frameworks Works for Beginners"

## Three load-bearing components
1. **Number** — specific count, not "many" or "tons of." The specificity is the credibility cue (Showing-Not-Telling at the title level).
2. **Strategy** — the specific method or system being credentialized.
3. **Audience** — the specific target demographic, particularly when paired with a counter-stereotype audience ("introverts," "beginners," "non-native English speakers"). The audience-specificity is what makes the title compelling to that audience.

## When to use
When the operator has earned the social proof. This formula is uniquely vulnerable to false-claim fatigue — using a number larger than what the operator can defend in the body of the video produces backlash.

## Why it works
Aggregated social proof activates the comparative-self mechanism (similar to the Bound Promise Formula) but at scale. The viewer auto-calculates: "If 147 people like me made this work, statistically I can too."

## Variants observed
Some operators use a "verified case study" approach ("Real student who [specific result]"); others use a milestone approach ("This system has now generated [aggregate outcome]"). The N-students stack form retained here is the highest-CTR variant when the social proof is real.
