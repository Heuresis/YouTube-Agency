# System 1 / System 2 Persuasion

## Origin archetype
Dual-process cognitive psychology (the fast/slow thinking research program and prospect-theory tradition) applied by long-form VSL specialists to high-ticket educational funnels.

## What it solves
Viewers run two cognitive engines. **System 1** — fast, automatic, always on — decides whether to click, whether to keep watching, whether to stay past the next ten seconds. **System 2** — slow, deliberate, expensive — decides whether to believe the mechanism, fill in the application, and buy. Assets designed only for System 1 get views and no applications. Assets designed only for System 2 earn respect and no watch time. Both must be engineered, in sequence, with the hand-offs placed where the brain actually makes them.

## Structure — the two systems

| | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Milliseconds | Seconds to minutes |
| Effort | Automatic | Deliberate, tiring |
| Capacity | Always on, parallel | Scarce, serial; defers to System 1 when exhausted |
| Error mode | Pattern-matching bias | Fatigue, then surrender to System 1 |
| Viewer role | Click, stay, feel | Evaluate, justify, commit |
| Channel role | CTR + retention | Mechanism belief + conversion |

**The core finding:** System 2 is lazy. Under fatigue, decision-load, or time pressure, it hands control back to System 1. Implication: the *close* moment — the end of a VSL, minute 35 of a sales call, the final screen of an application — must be System-1-friendly, because the audience is depleted there. The hardest cognitive work belongs early-to-middle, never at the end.

## Structure — hook duty (System 1) vs mechanism duty (System 2)

**Design for System 1:**
- Title + thumbnail (the 200ms sort)
- First 30 seconds of any video (INV-11's three mechanisms are System-1 devices)
- Pattern interrupts and re-hooks throughout the body
- The VSL's opening promise and its final CTA
- End screens and binge loops

**System 1 tactics:** specificity (one concrete number, one named artifact), contrast, vivid imagery, familiar-feeling narrative openings, motion/visual change, loss-framing.

**Design for System 2:**
- The mechanism walkthrough in the video's middle (logic ladders live here)
- Proof segments with verifiable artifacts on screen
- The VSL's offer-economics and comparison block
- The application form and its qualifying questions
- Sales-call scope and pricing discussion

**System 2 tactics:** numbered structure, tables and diagrams, exact figures with sources, before/after comparisons, calm pacing, deliberate white space (in speech: slowed delivery, fewer cuts).

## Structure — the four biases that decide high-ticket buys

1. **Anchoring.** The first number encountered shapes every later number-judgment. Operational: in the VSL, place the cost of the alternatives (a year of trial-and-error, the 1:1 consulting equivalent, the cost of staying stuck) *before* the program price, so the price lands against a meaningful anchor rather than against zero. The strongest anchor for an audience already losing something is the loss-rate, not a competitor's price tag. Every anchor must be true (INV-5, INV-6).
2. **Availability.** Probability is judged by how easily examples come to mind. Operational: three vivid, matched case stories outperform thirty data points; lead with the vivid story, keep the aggregate stats for the System-2 block. Counter a vivid objection ("my friend tried this and failed") with a vivid matched receipt, never with a statistic alone.
3. **Loss aversion.** Losses register roughly twice as hard as equivalent gains. Operational: "stop losing X" out-converts "gain X" at identical math — in hooks, in cost-of-delay framing, in guarantee language ("you keep the templates either way"). Constraint: loss-framing without demonstrated proof pattern-matches as fear-selling, and manufactured fear violates the trust contract (INV-16).
4. **Framing.** Logically equivalent statements produce different decisions. Operational frames that work: per-day cost framing, opportunity-cost framing ("the program costs less than the next six months of the current approach"), identity framing ("the kind of creator who runs a system, not a guessing loop"). Rule: **one frame per decision moment** — stacked frames in a single beat read as marketing copy and wake up skepticism.

## Structure — the fatigue curve, mapped

| Asset | Segment | Target system |
|---|---|---|
| Long-form video | 0:00–0:30 hook | System 1 |
| | 0:30–1:30 intro + credential | System 1 → 2 |
| | Body: mechanism + proof | System 2 peak (with System-1 re-hooks every 3–5 min) |
| | Final 60s: binge loop / CTA | System 1 |
| Pinned VSL | First 5 min: promise, story, stakes | System 1 |
| | Middle: mechanism, proof stack, economics | System 2 peak |
| | Close: calendar, ask, reassurance | System 1 |
| Sales call | First 5 min: rapport, recap | System 1 |
| | Minutes 5–25: scope, mechanism, fit | System 2 peak |
| | Final 10 min: story, decision, next step | System 1 |

Running a System-2 artifact at a close (a dense pricing table in the last two minutes) loses a depleted audience. Running System-1 material at the peak (eight minutes of pure story mid-VSL to a sophisticated audience) reads as evasive — they came for the mechanism.

## Diagnostic — which system is this speaking to?
Read the asset aloud and mark each paragraph **1** or **2**:
- Opening marked 2 (definitions, throat-clearing, "before we start") → dead hook; the viewer scrolled.
- Close marked 2 (tables, caveats, dense terms) → conversion leak; depleted viewers want the simple ask.
- Middle marked 1 throughout (all story, no mechanism) → sophisticated viewers disengage; Stage-3+ audiences came for the *how* (`market-sophistication-five-stages`).

A healthy long-form authority script alternates: 1 at every entry and exit, 2 in the teaching blocks, 1-flavored re-hooks stitching the 2-blocks together.

## Relationship to the Three-Brain Decision Sequence
`three-brain-decision-sequence` is the micro-sequence inside the first 15 seconds: Reptilian (0–3s) and Limbic (3–15s) are System-1 gates; the Neocortex (15s+) is System 2's entry point. This framework extends the same split across the *entire* asset and the *entire* funnel: where three-brain answers "what do the first 15 seconds do," System 1/System 2 answers "how is cognitive load allocated across the next 15 minutes — and across the VSL, the application, and the call." Use three-brain to build the hook; use this to architect everything after it.

## When to use
Hook engineering (`write-hook`), script architecture (`write-authority-script`), retention passes (`retention-engineer` places System-1 interrupts between System-2 blocks), VSL structure (`write-pinned-vsl`), application-funnel design, and sales-call scripting.

## Why it works
Attention and commitment are different brain budgets. System 1 spends freely and constantly; System 2 spends rarely and resents it. Content that asks for System-2 effort before System 1 has cleared safety-and-interest gets filtered unread; content that never engages System 2 never earns a justified decision — and high-ticket decisions that skip justification refund. Sequencing the two is the difference between a channel that entertains and a channel that converts.

## Anti-patterns
- **Essay-mode openings:** context-first, claim-later structure asks System 2 to work before System 1 agreed to stay.
- **Close-time complexity:** introducing new mechanism detail or new terms inside the final CTA window.
- **Frame-stacking:** three reframes of the price in one paragraph — each additional frame past the first subtracts trust.
- **Bias-only persuasion:** anchoring/loss/framing deployed to push a decision the belief layers don't support (`four-belief-layers`) produces buyers who churn — the System-2 audit happens *after* purchase, during refund windows.
- **Fear-frame escalation:** loss aversion ratcheted into doom-selling — violates INV-16 and trains the audience to discount every future claim.

## Variants observed
Some operators teach this as "lizard brain vs logic brain" (folded into three-brain) or as a hot/cold-state model. Canonical form retained: the dual-process framing, because it carries the fatigue-curve implication — the single most actionable rule (hard thinking early, easy deciding late) — which the simpler framings lose.
