# Hook Specimen 032 — AID opener for technical decisioning

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Beat 1 (0:00–0:05): Attention — name the specific high-stakes decision the viewer is facing. ("If you're choosing between [Tech A] and [Tech B] for [Use Case], here's what most teams get wrong.")
- Beat 2 (0:05–0:14): Interest — establish that the decision has hidden cost asymmetry. "These look similar on paper. They aren't. The wrong choice costs [specific consequence]."
- Beat 3 (0:14–0:22): Desire — bound-promise the decision framework. "I've helped [N] teams make this call. I'll walk you through the [N]-question filter we use."
- Beat 4 (0:22–0:30): Direction — set expectations. "This is technical, opinionated, and won't pretend both options are 'great in different contexts.'"

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
The AID variant for technical content earns retention by signaling stake-asymmetry early — Beat 2's "hidden cost" framing converts a casual decision into a high-stakes one in the viewer's mind. Beat 4's explicit anti-balanced framing is a reach pattern in technical audiences who are tired of weasel-worded "it depends" content. The promise of opinion is itself the value proposition.
