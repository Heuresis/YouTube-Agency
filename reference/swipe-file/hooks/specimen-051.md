# Hook Specimen 051 — Canonical-source authority opener

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Beat 1 (0:00–0:08): Reference a canonical source. "[Historical figure / canonical text] wrote about [domain] [N hundred / thousand years ago] and almost nobody applies what they said anymore."
- Beat 2 (0:08–0:16): Bridge to relevance. "What's strange is that the principle they identified is more useful today than it has ever been."
- Beat 3 (0:16–0:24): Position the creator as translator. "I've spent [N years] trying to apply this to modern [domain] and the results have been [specific outcome]."
- Beat 4 (0:24–0:35): Bound-promise. "I'll walk you through the principle, the modern application, and the worked example I'm running right now."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
The canonical-source opener borrows the gravity of established knowledge — the creator doesn't need to defend the idea's validity, only its modern application. Beat 2's "more useful today than ever" framing converts a historical claim into a contemporary opportunity. Positioning the creator as translator (Beat 3) rather than originator is a humility move that paradoxically increases authority.
