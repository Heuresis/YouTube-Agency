# Contrast-Format Specimen 009 — Stage-progression: $X-business vs $XX-business format

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "What you do at [low revenue tier] should be completely different from what you do at [high revenue tier]. Most operators apply the wrong tier's playbook."
- Tier 1 reality (2–3 min): What actually drives growth at the low tier. Specific actions. Common but rarely articulated.
- Tier 1 mistakes (1–2 min): What operators at this tier waste time on (often imitating higher-tier behaviors).
- Tier 2 reality (2–3 min): What changes at the next tier. Different decisions, different focus.
- Tier 2 mistakes (1–2 min): Where operators get stuck because they're still operating from tier-1 thinking.
- Transition triggers (1–2 min): "Here's how you know you're ready to shift from [Tier 1] to [Tier 2]."
- The bridge (1–2 min): The specific behaviors that need to change as you cross the threshold.
- Self-assessment (1 min): "Which tier are you actually in? And which tier's behaviors are you exhibiting?"

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
Stage-progression contrast formats earn solution-aware-audience retention by offering structural insight that's specific to where the viewer actually is — most business content is generic. The "applying the wrong tier's playbook" framing (Beat 1) is the load-bearing diagnosis; it names the specific failure mode the audience experiences without knowing what to call it. The self-assessment at the end (Beat: self-assessment) drives identification with the offer that solves their actual stage's problem.
