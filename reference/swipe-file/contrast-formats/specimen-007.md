# Contrast-Format Specimen 007 — Day-in-the-life: low-output vs high-output

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "I tracked two days last week. One was high-output. One was low-output. Same person, same job. Here's what I noticed."
- Low-output day timeline (3–4 min): Vlog-style walkthrough of the day. Show actual time-tracking. Honest about what happened in each block.
- Low-output reflection (1–2 min): What went wrong, structurally.
- High-output day timeline (3–4 min): Same vlog-style walkthrough. Show the deltas in real time.
- High-output reflection (1–2 min): What worked, structurally.
- The structural deltas (1–2 min): Side-by-side. Three to five specific differences. Each one explained.
- The principle (1–2 min): "What this taught me is [principle that applies beyond just this day]."
- Tactical entry point (1 min): "If you want to test this, do [specific small experiment] tomorrow."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
Day-in-the-life contrast formats earn retention because they convert abstract time-management advice into concrete observation — viewers can see what the differences look like in practice. The "same person, same job" framing (Beat 1) is the load-bearing element; without it, the contrast can be attributed to circumstance. The structural deltas beat (Beat 6) is what elevates the format from voyeurism to teaching.
