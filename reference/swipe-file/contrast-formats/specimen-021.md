# Contrast-Format Specimen 021 — High-saver vs high-spender behavior format

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "I tracked the spending of [N people] across the income spectrum. The savers and spenders aren't separated by income — they're separated by [N specific habits]."
- The income-equalizer (1–2 min): Show data that pairs of people at the same income level have wildly different savings rates.
- High-spender behavior 1 (1 min): Specific pattern with example.
- High-saver behavior 1 (1 min): The corresponding contrast pattern.
- Repeat for behaviors 2 through N (each 90s–2 min): Same structure.
- The compounding effect (1–2 min): "These small behavioral deltas, multiplied by [N years], create [extreme outcome gap]."
- The cognitive root (1–2 min): "All these behaviors trace back to one underlying belief about money."
- The shift (1–2 min): "If you want to migrate from spender to saver, the belief change matters more than the tactic change."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
High-saver-vs-high-spender contrast formats earn finance-audience retention because they prove that the wealth-gap is behavioral, not income-driven — a hopeful and actionable framing. The "income-equalizer" beat (Beat: income equalizer) is the load-bearing element; it pre-empts the "I just don't earn enough" objection. The cognitive-root beat (Beat: cognitive root) elevates the format from tactic-list to identity-shift.
