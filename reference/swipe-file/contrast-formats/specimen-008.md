# Contrast-Format Specimen 008 — What [identity A] does vs what [identity B] does

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "I want to show you the [N] specific things [identity A — the cohort the audience admires] do that [identity B — the cohort the audience is currently in] don't."
- Behavior 1 — Identity A (60–90s): Specific behavior, with example.
- Behavior 1 — Identity B (45–60s): What the contrast group does instead.
- Why the delta matters (45–60s): Mechanism explanation.
- Behaviors 2 through N (each 90s–2 min): Same structure.
- Pattern recognition (1–2 min): "Notice that [A] is operating on [meta-principle]. [B] is operating on [different meta-principle]."
- Identity-shift framing (1–2 min): "To move from [B] to [A], you don't need new tactics. You need a new stance."
- Entry-point (1 min): "Pick the one behavior that scares you the least. Do it this week."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
Identity-stratification contrast formats are the highest-leverage authority asset for high-ticket back-end offers because they install the identity-pivot that makes the offer feel necessary. The "you don't need new tactics, you need a new stance" framing (Beat: identity-shift) is the conversion-driving line; tactics are commoditized, identity-installation is not. The entry-point at the end (Beat: entry-point) prevents the ambitious framing from feeling unattainable.
