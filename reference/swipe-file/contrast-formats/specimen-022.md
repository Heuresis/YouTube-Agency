# Contrast-Format Specimen 022 — Speed vs quality test format

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "I built the same [thing] twice. Once optimizing for speed. Once for quality. The trade-off isn't what most people think."
- Speed-first build (3–4 min): Time spent, decisions made, corners cut.
- Speed-first result (1–2 min): What it actually looks like, what works, what doesn't.
- Quality-first build (3–4 min): Time spent, decisions made, refinements added.
- Quality-first result (1–2 min): Same structure.
- Direct comparison (1–2 min): Time, output, audience response.
- The non-obvious finding (1–2 min): "What surprised me was [counterintuitive variable] — the speed version actually [outperformed in unexpected dimension]."
- The contextual recommendation (1–2 min): "Speed wins for [conditions]. Quality wins for [conditions]. Most people pick wrong because [common reason]."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
Speed-vs-quality contrast formats earn retention because the audience faces this trade-off constantly and lacks structured evidence. The "non-obvious finding" beat (Beat: non-obvious finding) is the load-bearing curiosity element; without it, the format reads as a predictable trade-off explainer. Contextual recommendation prevents the video from becoming dogmatic, which the audience resists.
