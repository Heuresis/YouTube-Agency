# Contrast-Format Specimen 017 — Hypothesis-vs-reality test format

## Structure (paraphrased)
- Frame (0:00–0:30): "I had a hypothesis about [topic]. I designed a test. I was wrong about [specific aspect]."
- The hypothesis (1 min): What I expected to see and why.
- The test design (1–2 min): How I planned to evaluate the hypothesis.
- The execution (3–5 min): Going through the test in real time. On-camera.
- The data (1–2 min): What actually happened. On-screen results.
- The wrong-ness analysis (1–2 min): "Here's exactly where my hypothesis failed and what the data is actually showing."
- The corrected mental model (1–2 min): "What I think is true now is [updated belief]."
- Implications (1–2 min): "If I was wrong about [hypothesis], the practical implications are [consequences]."

## Why this works (the imitation lesson — 2–4 sentences)
Hypothesis-vs-reality contrast formats earn unusual trust because the creator is explicitly admitting prior wrongness — vulnerability paired with rigor is rare. The "wrong-ness analysis" beat (Beat: wrong-ness analysis) is load-bearing; without specific articulation of where the hypothesis failed, the format reads as performative humility. The corrected-mental-model beat converts the test into reusable insight.
