## Category descriptions

### Hooks (69 specimens)
The first 30 seconds of a long-form video — the structural opener the viewer's reptilian and limbic brains process before the rational brain engages. Each specimen captures a distinct cognitive ingress pattern. Hook formulas covered: PAST, AID, Contrast, Statistic, Contrarian, Bound-promise, Universal (curiosity stack), Famous-Figure-Reframe, Mentor-Reframe, Authority-stack, and several hybrids. The hooks/ folder also houses 15 loop-architecture and pattern-interrupt specimens (053–067) that apply across hook + body retention design.

### Intros (27 specimens)
Seconds 15–90 of a long-form video — the post-hook authority establishment, audience callout, content roadmap, objection pre-handle, and end-tease layer. The 90-second window is also the load-bearing slot for the soft 90-second CTA placement (intro specimen 003).

### Script sections (37 specimens)

Patterns include: re-hook → story → lesson, BUT-THEREFORE narrative, logic-ladder analytical, phase-by-phase blueprint, comparative decomposition, tactical walkthrough, belief-installation objection-handle, showing-not-telling with on-camera artifact, callback-driven continuity, five-emotion strategic-sequence, three-case-study social-proof stack, rhetorical-question complexity-strip, mini-case-study, source-citation, architecture-walkthrough, study-citation, deal-walkthrough, visibility-pattern, monetization-mechanism, documentary chapter, side-A-vs-side-B, framework-component, day-by-day challenge, belief-flip, step-by-step tactical, three-act story, objection-handling, emotional-arc, list-with-callback, wrong-vs-right, investigation-step, diagnostic-tree, high-cut-density, show-the-artifact, phased-timeline, and delayed-reveal.

### Outros (18 specimens)

## Cross-cutting specimen notes

The 262 specimens above represent the structural patterns extracted from corpus and world-class research, reformulated for use in an anonymized swipe file. Operators using these specimens should note:

1. **Performance bands are source-claimed.** No fact-check on the original videos is possible after anonymization. Treat all "elite" / "strong" / "top-1%" / "top-5%" tags as directional signals only — the structural pattern is the asset, not the specific performance claim.

2. **Specimens compose.** Most successful long-form videos use 1 hook specimen + 1 intro specimen + 4–8 script-section specimens + 1 outro specimen + 1 title specimen + 1 thumbnail specimen + (for multi-video arcs) 1 series structure. The composition is itself a creative act — the specimens are LEGO bricks, not finished structures.

3. **Mode (authority / reach) is the primary filtering axis.** When pulling specimens for a specific video, first filter by mode, then by archetype-fit, then by niche. Mixing reach hooks with authority outros produces a video that loses both audiences (too aspirational for converters, too direct-pitch for browsers).

4. **The watchlist applies to specimen output.** No operator names, no creator names, no channel names, no brand names appear in any specimen. Anyone editing or extending this swipe file must maintain that constraint.

5. **Specimens are starting points, not endpoints.** The structural pattern is reusable; the surface-level wording must be re-localized to the operator's voice, niche language, and audience cohort. A specimen used verbatim across operators reads as boilerplate; a specimen adapted to the operator's voice reads as authentic.

6. **Loop architectures and pattern-interrupt grammars (specimens 053–067 in hooks/) are cross-cutting.** They apply at the video-architecture level rather than the section level, and should be selected at planning time rather than after script drafting.
