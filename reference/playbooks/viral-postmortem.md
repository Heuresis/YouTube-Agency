# Viral Postmortem

> When a reach-mode video goes viral (defined as: views significantly above the channel's typical band, typically 5–20x the channel's median), the playbook for capitalizing: same-week follow-on content, community-tab response, email push, channel-architecture audit. The postmortem also runs the diagnostic: what made it viral, can it be repeated, and is the new audience composition durable or transient.

## What "viral" means in this OS

"Viral" is relative to the channel's baseline. A 50K-views video on a 100K-sub channel doing 3K-view averages is viral. A 1M-views video on a 100K-sub channel is exceptional. The structural marker: views > 5x channel median + algorithmic surfacing has continued past the typical 7-day window.

Most "viral" videos are reach-mode (the contrast-format viral specialist's pattern). Authority videos rarely go viral — they don't pull the broad audience that drives algorithmic acceleration. When an authority video does go viral, the postmortem has different implications (typically: the authority topic touched a broader nerve; the audience composition will normalize within 30 days).

## Why this playbook matters

A viral video is a brief gift. The window to capitalize is narrow — typically 7–14 days. After the window, algorithmic surfacing decays, audience interest fades, and the opportunity to monetize the spike closes. Operators who capitalize compound the gift; operators who don't lose most of the value to noise.

The capitalization is not "publish more videos like this immediately." It's:

1. Route the new audience correctly (via channel architecture)
2. Capture the audience's voice (for cycle-2 content)
3. Audit the channel architecture (does the spike route to the back-end?)
4. Convert the spike into durable owned audience (email, community)
5. Diagnose what made it work (for replication)

## Day 1–2 — Identify and orient

### Identify the viral threshold-break

Set up alerts (or daily check) for views > 5x channel median in 24 hours. When triggered:

- Note the video's title, thumbnail, hook, body, length
- Capture the analytics snapshot (views, watch time, AVD, CTR, traffic source breakdown)
- Note the comment volume and qualitative tone

### Orient

The first 48 hours are the spike's strongest. Do not panic-publish; do not panic-pivot. Orient first.

- **Is the audience composition matching the channel's target ICP?** Pull the demographics dashboard. If the spike is pulling viewers who are demographically off (wrong age, wrong geography, wrong interests), the spike is reach-mode-as-noise — it will not convert to back-end revenue.
- **What's the comment qualitative tone?** Are commenters expressing the channel's voice (audience match) or are they generic-comment noise (audience mismatch)?
- **What's the click pattern?** Where is the traffic coming from — Browse (algorithm-pushed), Search (specific-query), Suggested (next-to-related-video)? Browse-driven virality decays fastest; Search-driven virality compounds longer.

The orientation produces a decision: how aggressively to capitalize.

## Day 3–4 — Same-week follow-on content

Within 4–7 days, publish a follow-on video that builds on the viral video. Two patterns:

### Pattern A — The companion video

A separate video that addresses a question the viral video raised but didn't fully answer. End-screen of the viral video routes to the companion. Description of the viral video is updated to mention the companion. The companion catches the spillover audience.

### Pattern B — The deeper-dive video

The viral video introduces a topic; the deeper-dive expands. The deeper-dive is for the segment of the new audience that wants more, and is structurally stronger for routing them to the back-end (because the deeper-dive is more authority-mode and converts).

Both patterns require fast turnaround. Pre-recorded buffer content helps — if the operator has 2–3 unpublished videos in the buffer, one can be selected and adapted to be the follow-on. If no buffer exists, the operator must record and edit within 4–7 days, which is operationally heavy.

### Banned follow-on patterns

- **Re-uploading the viral video with a different title**: platform-policy violation
- **Publishing 3–5 derivative videos in a single week**: dilutes audience attention; algorithmic suppression
- **Pivoting the channel's positioning to the viral topic**: confuses the audience composition; long-term harm

The follow-on is a single targeted publish, not a flood.

## Day 3–7 — Community-tab response

The Community tab is the high-velocity response surface during a spike.

### Day 3: Acknowledgment post

A text post or image post acknowledging the spike. "Thank you for the response on [video]. Here's what you might want to know next." The post links to:

- The next-up video (if it's published or scheduled)
- The pinned VSL (for those wanting to go deeper)
- The channel's email list (for ongoing engagement)

### Day 4–5: Audience-research poll

A poll surfacing what the new audience wants next. "Which topic do you want me to cover from this conversation?" The responses feed `/idea-farm` for cycle-2 content.

### Day 6–7: Behind-the-spike post

A text post or image post sharing the spike from the operator's perspective — what they saw in the data, what they're thinking about, what's next. This is community-engagement infrastructure, building the parasocial trust with the new audience.

## Day 3–7 — Email push

The viral video typically generates 5–15x the channel's normal email-list growth in the spike window. The email list captures the most-converting subset of the new audience.

### Email push 1 (Day 3): Welcome to new subscribers

Send an email to new email-list subscribers acquired in the last 72 hours. Welcome them, frame the channel's positioning, link to the foundational playlist or the channel-thesis video. This onboards the spike-acquired subscribers.

### Email push 2 (Day 5–7): The deeper-dive

Send an email to the full email list (not just spike-acquired) referencing the viral moment and offering the deeper-dive content (could be the companion / deeper-dive video, a lead magnet, a free resource, or an invitation to the back-end offer). The email is the bridge from algorithmic spike to owned audience.

## Day 5–7 — Channel-architecture audit

The single biggest opportunity in a viral spike is whether the new audience is being routed to the back-end. Run the audit:

### End-screen audit

- Does the viral video's end-screen route to the next-step video? If not, fix it (the spike is still happening).
- Is the next-step video appropriate for the spike's audience composition? Reach-mode viral video → end-screen routes to authority video that's broad enough to retain the new audience without alienating them.
- Is the pinned VSL accessible? End-screen, channel banner, About section.

### Description audit

- Does the description link to the foundational playlist?
- Does the description link to the lead magnet?
- Does the description link to the pinned VSL (in a non-pushy way — typically positioned after the timestamps and main links)?

### Pinned-comment audit

- Is there a pinned comment? If not, add one immediately.
- Does the pinned comment route to a deeper-dive resource (a lead magnet, the foundational playlist, the pinned VSL)?
- Is the pinned-comment language calibrated to the spike's audience composition (broader than usual, less assumption of context)?

### Featured-section audit

- Is the channel's featured section showing the right videos for new visitors? Update to include the foundational playlist and channel-thesis video at top.

### About-section audit

- Does the channel's About section pitch the back-end offer (in a soft, value-rich way)? Is the email-list link at the top? Is the pinned VSL referenced?

The audit catches routing failures while the spike is live. Routing fixes done after the spike loses 60–80% of the leverage.

## Day 7–14 — Conversion measurement

By Day 7–14, the spike is decaying. Measurement begins:

### Application / call funnel measurement

- Applications received in the spike period (compared to baseline)
- Calls booked (compared to baseline)
- Cash collected (the lagging indicator)

The lag from view to application is typically 7–28 days; cash collected lags further. The full conversion measurement of a viral video isn't complete until 30–60 days post-spike.

### Email-list growth measurement

- Net new email subscribers in the spike period
- Welcome-sequence completion rate of spike-acquired subscribers (compared to baseline)
- Open rate / click rate of the email-push messages

### Subscriber growth measurement

- Net new YouTube subscribers in the spike period
- Subscriber retention curve at Day 30, 60, 90 (do they stay or churn?)

Spike-acquired subscribers typically have lower 90-day retention than organic-acquired subscribers. Plan for it.

## Day 14+ — Postmortem diagnosis

Once the spike has decayed, run the diagnosis. Why did the video go viral? Can it be repeated?

### Hook diagnosis

- What was the hook formula?
- What was the title pattern?
- What was the thumbnail composition?
- What was the first 30 seconds?

Run the hook against the channel's hook bank (compartment 6, hook & retention OS). Tier it up if the performance justifies (top-tier hook gets more weight in future video selection).

### Topic diagnosis

- What was the topic? Was it a recurring channel topic or a one-off?
- Did the topic touch a current event, a cultural moment, a niche-zeitgeist?
- Is the topic repeatable or is it a one-time spike?

### Structure diagnosis

- What was the video structure (contrast-format / contrarian / story / case-study / mechanism-first)?
- Did the structure carry retention or did the topic alone do it?
- What retention curve archetype fits the video?

### Audience composition diagnosis

- Was the audience composition durable or transient? (Transient: one-time spike viewers who don't stay subscribed. Durable: viewers who match the target ICP and convert to email / application.)
- The audience composition determines whether the video is the channel's new flagship or a one-off.

### Channel-architecture diagnosis

- Did the channel architecture route the spike correctly?
- What routing failures surfaced (broken end-screen, missing pinned comment, weak description link, etc.)?
- What architectural changes are needed for the next spike?

### Replication strategy

Based on the diagnosis, decide:

- **Replicate the hook + topic combination**: produce 1–2 similar videos in the next 60 days, calibrating to maintain quality and avoid audience fatigue.
- **Replicate the structure but vary the topic**: the structure is the lever, not the topic. Apply the same structure to other channel-relevant topics.
- **Treat the spike as a one-off**: the topic was timely / cultural / non-repeatable. Capture the audience but don't pivot the channel.

The replication strategy is informed by the diagnosis, not by the spike's view count alone.

## Cycle-2 follow-up content

Plan 1–3 follow-on videos in the next 60 days that build on the viral moment:

- **Follow-on 1** (Week 2–3): the same-week companion or deeper-dive (already published per Day 3–4 step)
- **Follow-on 2** (Week 4–6): a related topic that builds on the audience the spike acquired. Tone calibrated to the durable subset of the spike audience.
- **Follow-on 3** (Week 7–10): an offer-adjacent video that converts the durable audience to the back-end. Pinned-VSL reference; application CTA.

The follow-on sequence is a mini-series that captures the spike's compounding value.

## Failure modes

### Panic-publish

**Failure**: the operator publishes 3–5 derivative videos in the spike week. The audience is overwhelmed; the algorithm suppresses; the spike's compounding is undermined.
**Prevention**: one targeted follow-on per Day 3–4 step. Resist the temptation.

### Pivot-channel

**Failure**: the operator pivots the channel's positioning to chase the viral topic. The existing audience disengages; the new audience doesn't sustain; the channel loses both.
**Prevention**: the channel's positioning is upstream of any single video. Run `/build-niche` if the operator is genuinely considering a positioning shift; do not pivot reactively.

### Routing-architecture neglect

**Failure**: the spike routes to nowhere. Pinned VSL missing or buried. End-screen routing wrong. Description without lead-magnet link. The spike's audience watches one video and bounces.
**Prevention**: Day 5–7 architecture audit. The audit catches routing failures while the spike is live.

### Comment-engagement neglect

**Failure**: the operator doesn't engage with comments during the spike. The new audience perceives absence; the parasocial-trust contract doesn't form.
**Prevention**: first-hour-only engagement strategy applies during the spike. The operator (or community manager) engages with high-signal comments in the first 24 hours.

### Email-push miss

**Failure**: the new email subscribers don't receive a welcome email. They forget the channel; they don't return.
**Prevention**: welcome-sequence automation. Day 3 welcome push is non-negotiable.

### Diagnosis skip

**Failure**: the spike happens; the operator celebrates; no diagnosis runs. The next viral attempt fails; the operator doesn't know why.
**Prevention**: Day 14+ postmortem is non-negotiable. The diagnosis feeds compartment 6 + library compound.
